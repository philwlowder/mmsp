Enigma Outbreak

posted: 2/18/14

On February 19th, 1884 the Great Southeastern Tornado Outbreak, also known as the Enigma Outbreak, swept across the Southeastern United States. This event resulted in the name Naked Mountain for what later became Morrow Mountain.

Where the Uwharrie River joins the Yadkin River, the two form the Pee Dee. John Kirk and his descendants owned the ferry that crossed along this dividing line of Yadkin and Pee Dee. The ferry landed on the west side of the river at what is now Morrow Mountain State Park boat access. Along with the ferry, the Kirk family owned an inn. The Kirk Inn sat on a hill above the the ferry landing. The site is now crossed by the park's Three Rivers Trail. From that hill one can look across the Yadkin-Pee Dee and up the Uwharrie.

In 1883, David Tillman Lowder bought the Kirk Inn and ferry. On the day of February 19th, 1884 the Kirk Inn was the new home of David and Ellen Lowder. The storm began about 7:30 pm in the dark of that February night and it soon destroyed the Kirk Inn. Ellen Austin Lowder and two daughters were injured. It would have been a long dark night with heavy rains and lightning accompanying the winds. The injured were taken to the Kron home and medical facilities and were cared for there.

From the Carolina Watchman February 28th, 1884

Ellen Austin Lowder died a few days later as a result of her injuries. Two daughters, Becky and Josephine, were badly injured. Becky had a broken jawbone and Josephine had a broken hip.*

The Carolina Watchman February 28th, 1884 reported on the storm. In the article's second column there is a mention of the "plantiff cries" coming from the Kirk property that was across the river in Stanly County. Those cries would have been from the Lowder family after the Kirk Inn was destroyed. Here is a section of that paper that provides an eyewitness account of that night. The witness spent the night near the mouth of the Uwharrie River.

And of course the trees on top of Morrow Mountain were blown down leaving it naked.

* Information about these injuries is taken from page 62 of Our Children's Heritage by Alberta Lowder Boggan.

by Phil W. Lowder
